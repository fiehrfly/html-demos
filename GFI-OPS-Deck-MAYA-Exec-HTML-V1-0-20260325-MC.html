Why This Matters Now

Before We Go Further

Think of a change you resisted.
The reason it felt wrong is the same reason rollouts fail.

It was not the change itself. It was the unfamiliarity of it. The same instinct that made you hesitate is the instinct your team brings to every change you introduce.

Resistance is almost never about whether the change is correct. It is about whether it feels safe enough to follow. MAYA is the gap between those two things.

MAYA in Action

Even the world's most powerful companies have had to submit to what people already know.

The QWERTY keyboard and Apple's tactile bump reversal prove that correctness never beats familiarity -- adoption does.

1

The Keyboard You Use Right Now

QWERTY was never the best layout. It was the most familiar one.

Built for machines, not people The QWERTY layout was designed around physical typewriter mechanics to prevent arm jams -- not to optimize human typing. It was never the best option. It was the most buildable one at the time.
Why it survived By the time better layouts existed, hundreds of millions of people had already learned QWERTY. The familiarity was irreversible. No amount of logic could overcome what people already knew in their hands.
Apple submitted to the same force Apple tried moving the tactile finger-placement bumps from F and J to D and K. Ergonomically justified. Logically sound. The market convention was too strong. They reversed course. Even Apple, with its full design authority, had to respect what people already knew.
2

Social Media App Updates

The same update. Two rollouts. Two completely different outcomes.

When MAYA is applied You barely notice the interface changed. Navigation shifted slightly. A button moved. You are already using the new features before you realize you adapted. No friction. No complaints. Adoption happens invisibly.
When MAYA is skipped Users post complaints, rate the app one star, demand the old version back, and generate entire social media movements -- then keep using it anyway. The product did not break. The familiarity did.
What this proves Resistance is not evidence that the change was wrong. It is evidence that the rollout did not carry people across the gap between what they knew and what you were asking them to do.
!

What Apple proved with its effort to differentiate: MAYA is not timidity. It is strategy. Even the world's most powerful companies have had to answer to what people already know. Correctness never beats familiarity. Adoption always wins.

The MAYA Spectrum

Most rollouts land in the resistance zone without anyone realising it.

The difference between a change that sticks and one that stalls is rarely the idea -- it is where the rollout sits on this spectrum.

Target Zone 75 to 85%
0% -- No MAYA (Shock Change) 100% -- Full MAYA (No Real Change)
0 to 40%

Too Aggressive

No familiar anchors. Resistance is immediate and emotional even when the change is correct and necessary. Credibility takes a hit even if the idea survives.

50 to 85%

Adoption Sweet Spot

Enough innovation to move forward. Enough familiarity that people land with you. Mandatory changes can still hit this range with the right framing and sequencing.

85 to 100%

Too Cautious

Language of change present, but actual change so diluted that no meaningful progress happens. Comfort preserved, problem preserved with it.

Not every change hits 75%. Regulatory mandates have fixed urgency. But even there, MAYA thinking reduces resistance. The spectrum is not a judgment. It is a calibration tool. Show continuity wherever it exists and you move the needle even on non-negotiable change.

Team Activity

Four rollouts. One question for each: what would you have done differently?

Click each scenario to see how MAYA thinking changes the outcome.

Scenario 01

A new digital form replaces a paper-based process. No training is provided. The launch email says: "Starting Monday, use the new system."

Where does this land? What is missing?

This lands around 20 to 30%. The rollout skipped every familiar anchor. A side-by-side comparison with the old form, even just one walkthrough session, would move this toward 65%+ with no change to the underlying requirement.

Scenario 02

A compliance update arrives as a 12-page PDF. Subject line: "IMPORTANT: Please read and acknowledge by Friday."

What does this feel like to receive? What would change the outcome?

The content may be mandatory but the delivery creates friction. A one-page "what changed vs. what stayed the same" summary and a brief team walkthrough would dramatically improve adoption without altering the requirement itself.

Scenario 03

A new project management tool is introduced with a demo, a comparison to the current process, and a two-week parallel run before full cutover.

What MAYA elements are present? Why does this work?

This sits around 75 to 80%. The parallel run and comparison are strong familiar anchors. People see continuity between old and new before they are required to let go of what they know. This is MAYA applied intentionally.

Scenario 04

A significant agent-facing change is tested internally for six months, then rolled out to agents framed as a "small update" to avoid alarming them.

Is this MAYA, or is something else happening here?

MAYA is about familiarity and framing -- not concealment. If the framing hides the real scope of a change, trust erodes faster than resistance ever would. MAYA works with transparency. The goal is to make change feel manageable, not invisible.

The Ops Standard

Three questions that prevent resistance before it starts.

Every change request that moves through Operations -- regardless of source or urgency -- passes through this lens first. These are not gates. They are the reason the right changes actually move.

Question 01

What does this replace or sit next to that the team already knows?

Click to reveal why it matters →

Why it matters

Skipping this question is why rollouts get derailed by questions that should have been answered before the meeting. If you cannot name the familiar anchor, the change has no bridge. People will not build one themselves.

Question 02

What is the smallest familiar anchor we can connect this change to?

Click to reveal why it matters →

Why it matters

One strong anchor outperforms five weak ones -- and it takes less time to find it than to manage the resistance it prevents. Find the thing people already trust, already do, or already understand.

Question 03

What would make this feel new but not foreign?

Click to reveal why it matters →

Why it matters

The same change can feel like a disruption or an evolution. That choice belongs to whoever designs the rollout. MAYA is often a communication decision as much as a design one.

Explore It Yourself

The next change you introduce will be judged on this.

Five questions to close the gap between knowing MAYA and applying it.

1

Think of a change you resisted at work. What made it feel too unfamiliar -- and what would have made it easier to accept?

2

Recall a tool or update you adopted quickly. What made the transition feel natural? Was MAYA at work without anyone naming it?

3

If you were rolling out a new workflow today, what familiar anchor would you use to introduce it -- and how would you frame the rollout?

4

Think of a change coming to your team in the next 90 days. What familiar anchor could make it land better, before the rollout even begins?

5

If you had to introduce a change today with zero resistance, what would that rollout look like -- and how close are we to that right now?
